Unit 2 · Key Concepts

Types of Workplace Hazards

Click the accordion items below to learn details about each type of hazard. This is ADAPT's Accordion Component.

Noise, vibration, extreme temperatures, radiation, and slipping/tripping are included in this category. These are the most common workplace hazards. They can be reduced by wearing PPE (Personal Protective Equipment) and maintaining safe walkways.
Toxic gases, corrosive liquids, flammable materials and carcinogens are included in chemical hazards. Always read SDS (Safety Data Sheet) and ensure proper storage. Get training before handling any chemical.
Bacteria, viruses, fungi and parasites are biological hazards. These are more common in healthcare and food industries. Regular hand washing, proper sanitization and PPE use protect against biological hazards.
Repetitive motion, poor posture, heavy lifting and uncomfortable workstations create ergonomic hazards. Adjustable chairs, keyboard placement and taking regular breaks help reduce these hazards. This is very important to prevent musculoskeletal disorders.
Work stress, bullying, harassment and workload imbalance are psychosocial hazards. Talking to HR, using counseling services and creating an open communication culture help manage these hazards.

💡Remember

Hazard ≠ Risk. Hazard is something that can cause harm. Risk is the probability of that harm occurring. Identifying hazards is the first step, risk assessment comes after.

Unit 3 · Step-by-Step

Emergency Response Steps

Navigate the slides using arrows. This is ADAPT's Narrative Component — it shows steps one after another.

🚨

Step 1: Assess the Situation

In any emergency, first check the safety of yourself and others. Don't panic. Determine if the scene is safe or not. If the scene is unsafe, stay away and call the authorities. Before taking any action, pause for a second and think.

📞

Step 2: Alert & Call for Help

Call emergency services immediately (112 or company helpline). Clearly state your name, location and situation. Stay on the phone until the operator says you can hang up. Also alert a colleague.

🚑

Step 3: First Aid (If Trained)

If you are trained in first aid, provide basic help. Control bleeding, perform CPR if needed. Don't move anyone forcefully — especially if spinal injury is suspected. You should know the location of the first aid kit beforehand.

🏃

Step 4: Evacuate if Needed

In case of fire or gas leak, evacuate the building immediately. Use emergency exits — don't use the lift. Go to the muster point and report attendance. Report to your supervisor or safety officer. Don't leave anyone behind.

📝

Step 5: Report & Document

Every incident — no matter how small — must be reported. Fill out the incident report form, write the date/time/location and note the names of witnesses. This helps prevent similar accidents in the future and is also a legal requirement.

Protect hands from cuts, burns, chemicals and vibration. Select gloves based on the type of hazard — chemical resistant, cut resistant, or thermal gloves.

Safety Boots
Steel toe boots protect feet from heavy objects. Anti-slip soles prevent slipping. Insulated boots are required in areas with electrical hazards.
